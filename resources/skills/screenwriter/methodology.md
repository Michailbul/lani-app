# Methodology

Three books we rely on constantly.

---

## Robert McKee — "Story"

### Controlling Idea
One sentence in the form "Life becomes X when the hero does Y, because Z". This is the film's controlling idea. Every scene must serve it.

If you cannot state the controlling idea in one sentence — the film has no spine. The idea first, then the scenes.

### Scene value (value movement)
In any scene, **something changes** — from + to – or the reverse. A value enters the scene in one state and leaves in another.

**A scene with no turn of value = not a scene.** It is just description.

Before writing a scene, answer:
- What value enters it? (For example: trust.)
- In what state? (For example: the team trusts the leader.)
- In what state does it leave? (For example: the team suspects the leader.)
- How does this happen? (What action flips the value.)

Common value pairs:
- Control ↔ helplessness
- Trust ↔ betrayal
- Love ↔ obsession
- Life ↔ death
- Freedom ↔ captivity
- Discipline ↔ chaos
- Team ↔ instrument
- Hope ↔ despair
- Knowledge ↔ ignorance

### Inciting Incident
The event that knocks the hero's life out of balance. It must happen **in the first quarter** of the film (for a 90-minute film — before minute 22).

Without this event the plot does not start. The hero's life BEFORE this event and AFTER are two different worlds.

### Progressive Complications
Each next conflict is stronger than the last. The gap between the hero's expectation and the result of his action keeps growing.

If your third-act conflict is weaker than the first — the structure is broken.

### Crisis → Climax → Resolution
- **Crisis:** the hero's final dilemma. A choice between two incompatible values. The hardest point.
- **Climax:** the action that answers the dilemma. The most physical and loudest moment in the film.
- **Resolution:** a short coordinate of the new world after the climax.

### The "+" and "–" pendulum
The hero cannot do well the whole time or do badly the whole time. Alternation. With each iteration the amplitude grows.

In a fall arc the "+"s become local (small wins), the "–"s become systemic (large losses).
In a rise arc it is the reverse.

### Show, don't tell
Instead of inner monologues — actions and objects. An object in frame says more than a line.

A pregnancy test instead of the line "she's pregnant".
A torn letter instead of the line "he broke up with her".
An empty chair instead of the line "he is alone".

## Joseph Campbell — "The Hero with a Thousand Faces"

Use Campbell **as a diagnostic**, not as dogma. Not every story is a pure hero's journey. This is especially true if you are writing an anti-hero or a tragedy (then the mirrored journey applies — *katabasis* with no return, like Orpheus going after Eurydice).

### The 12 stages

1. **Ordinary World** — the hero in balance. Show what exists so the viewer understands what will be lost.
2. **Call to Adventure** — an event that demands the hero leave home.
3. **Refusal of the Call** — the hero hesitates, is afraid. Without this stage the hero is not a hero but a function.
4. **Meeting the Mentor** — someone gives a tool, a skill, or knowledge. Can be a false mentor = the shadow.
5. **Crossing the Threshold** — the hero crosses the border between worlds.
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** — settling into the new world.
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** — preparation for the main ordeal.
8. **The Ordeal** — the encounter with death or with the shadow.
9. **The Reward** — the hero gets what he came for.
10. **The Road Back** — the return to the ordinary world (or, for an anti-hero, the impossibility of returning).
11. **Resurrection** — the final test. The hero dies and is reborn.
12. **Return with the Elixir** — the hero brings something new to the world.

### Archetypes
- **Hero:** the active engine of the plot.
- **Shadow:** the anti-hero, a reflection of the hero's dark side.
- **Mentor:** gives a tool.
- **Threshold Guardian:** an obstacle at the entrance to the new world.
- **Herald:** announces the call.
- **Shapeshifter:** switches sides.
- **Trickster:** a comic character who breaks the rules.

In an anti-heroic tragedy the **mentor is often = the shadow**. The hero does not realize that his teacher is his enemy.

## Aristotle — "Poetics"

### Ἁμαρτία (hamartia) — the hero's fatal flaw
Not "sin" in a moral sense, but a flaw in character that sets the tragedy in motion. A good hero has a weak trait that in ordinary life is a virtue, and in an exceptional situation is a catastrophe.

"I love too much" is the hamartia in a romantic tragedy.
"I trust everyone" — in a political one.
"I cannot forgive" — in a family one.

The hamartia must be **visible from the first scene** and **detonate at the climax**.

### Περιπέτεια (peripeteia) — the reversal of action into its opposite
An action that should have led to X leads to –X.

Wanting to save, he kills. Wanting to protect, he destroys. Wanting to open, he closes forever.

A strong film has 2–3 peripeteias, escalating in force.

### Ἀναγνώρισις (anagnorisis) — recognition
The moment when the hero (or the viewer) understands what was hidden.

The best anagnorisis is the one that **coincides with the peripeteia**. A double blow: the hero learns the truth at the same moment his action turns into its opposite.

### Κάθαρσις (catharsis) — purification through fear and pity
The ending must give the viewer a mixed feeling of sympathy for the hero and fear for themselves ("I could have been him").

### Unity of action
Aristotle demands that everything in the tragedy lead to one point. Every action, every object, every line must serve the final point.

**If a scene does not serve the final point — it is unnecessary.** The test is simple: remove the scene — does the film change? If not — cut it.

## HOW TO USE THIS

Before writing each scene:
1. What is the controlling idea (from McKee)?
2. What value enters this scene and what value leaves?
3. Which Campbell stage is this? (If applicable.)
4. How does the scene serve the hero's hamartia?
5. Is this a peripeteia scene or a setup scene?

If the answers are empty — the scene is either unnecessary or needs to be rethought.

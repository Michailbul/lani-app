# Writing Rules

## GENERAL RULES

1. **Action verbs. No descriptions.**
   The camera shoots only what is seen and heard. No "he feels", "he understands", "it rushes through his head", "the atmosphere is tense". Only verbs (HITS, EXITS, GRABS, LOOKS, WHISPERS).

2. **Brevity = talent.** If you can say it in two words — say it in two words. An extra line = an extra minute of film.

3. **Change only what is asked.** A targeted Edit, with no "improvement" of neighboring lines.

4. **Hollywood format.**
   - Slug line: ALL CAPS, bold, left margin
   - Action: left margin, present tense, only verbs of action
   - Character cue: ALL CAPS, indent ≈ 2.2" from the margin (3168 DXA)
   - Parenthetical: indent ≈ 1.6" from the margin (2304 DXA)
   - Dialogue: indent 1" left + 1.5" right (1440 / 2160 DXA)
   - Transition: right margin, ALL CAPS

5. **One version, not five.** When you propose a scene — you give one version + an argument. If the user rejects it, you ask a narrow binary question and give the next single version.

6. **Slug-line format.** `INT./EXT. — LOCATION — TIME OF DAY`. Time of day is usually: DAWN / DAY / EVENING / NIGHT. The slug line is ALWAYS in one language (either English or Russian, chosen at the start of the project).

---

## BAD vs GOOD

❌ "A grey dawn paints the mountain peaks in pinkish tones. An ancient temple rises over the abyss."

✅ "EXT. MOUNTAINS — DAWN. Three dots race over the snowy ridge."

---

❌ "The hero feels his heart tearing apart from the pain of loss. His eyes are full of longing."

✅ "The hero LOOKS at the mountain." (What he feels, the actor plays.)

---

❌ "The teacher, with a mysterious smile, speaks in a mysterious voice that carries centuries of wisdom:"

✅ "TEACHER: «You already know.»"

---

❌ "A harsh, bloody battle rages, in which the hero fights for his life, baring his resolve."

✅ "The hero HITS. The enemy FALLS. The hero TURNS. A second enemy is ALREADY BEHIND HIM."

## CHARACTER SPEECH IDENTITY

Every main character must have a **recognizable voice**. Read a line with no name attached — it should be clear who is speaking.

Voice parameters:
- **Line length** — short/long.
- **Register** — high/low, literary/colloquial.
- **Slang** — present/absent, which kind.
- **Self-assessment in speech** — does the character make excuses, does he admit things.
- **Silence** — who stays silent more than the others? Silence is also a trait.
- **Patterns** — which phrases recur for this character?

A good device: give each character a **unique way of addressing** the same other character. One uses the name. Another uses a nickname. A third never addresses them directly.

---

## RHYTHM AND PACE

- **Fight / chase** — short verb lines. One verb per line. A clipped rhythm.

  ```
  The hero HITS.
  The enemy FALLS.
  The hero RAISES the sword.
  ```

- **Dialogue** — short lines, pauses through action ("The hero is silent", not "the hero says nothing because he doesn't know what to say").

- **Descriptive scenes** — also through action, not through describing a state.

  ❌ "The atmosphere grew more and more tense."
  ✅ "The light goes out. Only breathing is heard."

---

## AVOID

- **Inner monologues of characters.** If it can be shown through action — show it.
- **Landscape descriptions longer than one line.** One shot — one line.
- **Mood adjectives** ("anxious", "ominous", "mysterious"). That is the viewer's mood, not the screenwriter's.
- **Explaining things to the viewer directly through dialogue** ("as you remember, we are close relatives, and our mother was killed in 1992"). This is an "info dump" — a failure.
- **Lines that repeat what is already visible in frame.** If the hero cries — you do not need the line "I'm sad".

---

## SUPPORT

- **Objects as mines.** Every appearance of an object in the film must move the plot. If you showed a gun in the first act — it must fire in the third (Chekhov's rule). If you showed it twice — the third time you are obligated to use it.

- **Repeated lines.** The same line, spoken in different contexts, becomes a meaning-mine. "I love you" at the start of the film and "I love you" before a killing are different lines.

- **Silence.** A character's silence at a moment when the viewer expects a line is the loudest sound in the film.

- **Symmetry.** The first scene and the last scene should rhyme. Through an object, a location, a line, or an action.

# Screen Time and Cutting Length

## THE BASIC RULE

"1 page of Hollywood format ≈ 1 minute of screen time" is an **averaged** rule. It works for a mixed dialogue/action screenplay. It does **NOT** work literally for action-heavy and montage scenes.

---

## SCENE TYPES BY SPEED

### Very fast (5–15 seconds)

- **Montage scenes** — 5–10 short action lines running as a visual flow. On the page — half a page, on screen — 8–12 seconds.
- **Establishing shots** — a shot of a location with no dialogue, no action. 3–5 seconds.
- **Match-cut transitions** — 2–3 seconds.

An 8–10 second example:

```
INT. ARMORY — DAY
The door OPENS.
The walls are hung with swords.
QUICK MONTAGE:
— A hand grabs a spear.
— A sword leaves its scabbard.
— Bare feet run down a corridor.
```

This is half a page — 10 seconds of screen time.

### Fast (15–45 seconds)

- **Action beats with short lines** — a chase with one line, a single skirmish.
- **Cutaway scenes** — a short return to a parallel storyline.

### Medium (45 seconds — 1:30)

- **Dialogue with action** — 2–3 lines + a physical action.
- **Breathing scenes** — a moment of silence before or after a climax.

### Long (1:30 — 3:00)

- **Dramatic dialogue with a turn** — a confrontation, a confession, a breakup.
- **Action sequence** — chase + fight + rescue.
- **A complex scene with several beats** — setting a task, conflict, resolution.

### Very long (3:00 — 7:00)

- **Climax** — the final fight with a moral choice, all storylines converge.
- **Big set-piece** — a staged segment with repeated changes of pace.

---

## HOW TO COUNT SCREEN TIME

Not by pages. By beats inside each scene.

**Algorithm for a single scene:**

1. **Slug line + establishing** — 3–5 seconds (or 0, if a matched cut from the previous scene).
2. **Each action line separately:**
   - Simple action (1 verb, 1 object): 1–2 seconds.
   - Complex action (several movements in one line): 3–5 seconds.
   - Large physical event (explosion, fall, transformation): 5–10 seconds.
3. **Each dialogue line:** ~150 words per minute average pace = 2.5 words per second.
   - Short line "Go." — 1 second.
   - Medium "The plan in the sanctum — lower floor, the back side, quiet." — 4–5 seconds.
   - Long (10+ words) — 6–10 seconds.
4. **Pauses and silence** — counted separately. "A long pause" = 3–5 seconds minimum.
5. **Reaction shots** (no words and no large actions) — 2–3 seconds each.

Add it all up — you get the real screen time of the scene.

---

## RE-CHECK

If the calculated time diverges sharply from your gut feeling — recount. People more often err toward overestimating length (they think "this is a long scene", but on screen it is 30 seconds).

A good indicator: watch a similar scene in any reference film with a stopwatch. Compare it with what you have written.

## CUTTING PLAN TO A TARGET LENGTH

When the user wants to "fit into X minutes":

1. **Count the current time** by scene (a table).
2. **Determine the difference** "what you have − what you need".
3. **Sort scenes by cutting priority:**
   - Lowest cutting priority (= best to delete): repetitions, parallel beats of the same thing, excess reaction shots.
   - Medium priority: long breathing scenes, extended landscape shots.
   - Do not touch: the climax, peripeteias, anagnorisis, key turning points.

---

## COMMON CUT POINTS (from safe to risky)

### Safe (can be cut with no loss)

1. **Duplicate beats** — the same emotion repeated in three scenes. Keep the strongest, delete the rest.
2. **Parallel beats** — three blows of a battering ram, four times the hero tries, five crowd reactions. Reduce to 2 iterations (the rule of threes: shown-shown-period).
3. **Lines that duplicate action** — the hero cries and says "I'm sad". Delete the line.
4. **Transitional scenes with no turn of value** — the hero travels from location A to location B, the conversation does not move the plot.

### Medium (cut carefully)

5. **Long parametric descriptions** — detailed descriptions of costumes, locations. Compress to 1 line.
6. **Extended actions** — the hero does three things where one is enough. Keep the main one.
7. **In-scene reaction shots** — 4 reaction faces in a montage, compress to 2.

### Risky (only if truly necessary)

8. **An entire comedic or emotional cutaway scene.** It can weaken the rhythm, but it adds a minute.
9. **A character's subplot.** If the hero has a secondary arc — it can be trimmed. But not removed entirely.

### Never touch

10. **The Inciting Incident.**
11. **The peripeteias.**
12. **The anagnorisis.**
13. **The crisis dilemma.**
14. **The climax.**
15. **The setup of mine-objects** that will fire later.

---

## THE "IF YOU REMOVE IT — DOES THE FILM CHANGE?" RULE

The test for every scene: remove it mentally. Does anything change in the film's ending?

- **Yes, it changes** → the scene is needed.
- **No, it does not change** → the scene is unnecessary. Cut it with no regret.
- **Only the tone/mood changes** → the scene is a breathing scene. It can be cut if you are short on minutes, but it is a loss.

---

## BILINGUAL SCREENPLAYS AND TIME

If the screenplay is bilingual (dialogue EN + RU caption, or the reverse) — **the doubled dialogue does not count toward screen time**. Count only the main language. The translation is for reading, not for shooting.

When estimating the length of a bilingual document: the page count is +1–2 pages because of the translations, but the real screen time is the same as the monolingual version.
